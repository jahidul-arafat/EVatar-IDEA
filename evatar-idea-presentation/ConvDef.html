Auto-Enhance vs AffectNet — why the comparison is valid

Three concrete tag-placement examples on the same sentence — then the unified pipeline flowchart showing how all three are scored identically.

REVIEWER CHALLENGE
"You criticise Auto-Enhance for lacking AffectNet validation — but you also use AffectNet to score your algorithm. If AffectNet is the arbiter, why is that a criticism rather than just a different design choice?"
In plain English — imagine a cooking competition where both contestants are judged by the same panel of expert tasters.

Auto-Enhance cooks a dish, hands it to the judges, and hears the score for the first time. It had no idea what the judges valued — sweetness, texture, presentation — before cooking.

EVAtAR-IDEA spent 64 rounds tasting its own dish with a portable version of the same judging panel, adjusting the recipe each time based on the score, before the final judged meal.

When both dishes are scored at the end by the same judges: yes, the same scoring panel is used. But one chef cooked blind. The other used the scoring criteria as a training signal from the start. The reviewer is asking whether that difference matters — it does, because it is exactly what the research is designed to measure.

🛡️
Elitism — score can never go down
The top 2 scorers from each generation are carried forward unchanged. If all 8 children in Gen 2 score worse than Gen 1's best, that Gen 1 winner survives into Gen 3. p* is the global best seen across all 64 renders — not just the Gen 8 winner.
📈
Convergence guarantee
F(p*) is monotonically non-decreasing across generations. A dip in one generation's average score is fine — elite survivors prevent any regression. Paper 2 reports per-generation convergence curves to verify this empirically.
Every candidate at every generation is rendered and scored. AffectNet is the live feedback signal driving selection — not a post-hoc judge. Cost: 64 × $0.048 = $3.07 per sentence.
The reviewer's confusion
Both approaches produce a final video clip that is scored by your DeepFace + AffectNet pipeline — so yes, the same measurement tool judges both outputs.

But the reviewer conflates two different roles AffectNet can play:

Role A — Evaluator (post-hoc): You render a clip, then run AffectNet to see how well it scored. Auto-Enhance only ever experiences AffectNet in this role. It placed tags, rendered once, and the score arrived as a surprise. AffectNet had no influence on how tags were chosen.

Role B — Objective function (inside the loop): You run AffectNet during the design process, using its score as a signal to improve the next candidate. EVAtAR-IDEA uses AffectNet in this role — 64 times per sentence, at every generation, shaping which tag sequences survive and which are discarded.

Criticising Auto-Enhance for not using Role B is not the same as saying AffectNet is a biased judge. It is saying: Auto-Enhance never used the feedback signal that EVAtAR-IDEA was built around. That gap — between being evaluated by a measure and being optimised toward it — is precisely what the research quantifies.

THE FIVE ARGUMENTS
ARGUMENT 01
Same scorer, different generators
Auto-Enhance returns a tagged script only — no score, no feedback. You apply DeepFace identically to all three.
ARGUMENT 02
Empirical, not architectural, criticism
Auto-Enhance fails because its observed positions — 65–80% — trigger F3 late-onset. Measurable, not assumed from its internals.
ARGUMENT 03
Optimises TOWARD vs evaluated BY
EVAtAR-IDEA runs DeepFace 64× per sentence inside its loop. Auto-Enhance runs AffectNet zero times. One studied the marking scheme.
ARGUMENT 04
Practice-facing comparison
Auto-Enhance is what practitioners actually use. Does evolutionary optimisation beat the commercial default by a pre-registered, significant margin?
ARGUMENT 05
Pre-registered at OSF
All three baselines and the scoring protocol were declared at OSF before any data collection. No post-hoc cherry-picking.

CITED REFERENCE
AffectNet: A Database for Facial Expression, Valence, and Arousal Computing in the Wild
Mollahosseini, A., Hasani, B., & Mahoor, M. H. · IEEE Transactions on Affective Computing, 10(1), 18–31 · 2019
DOI: 10.1109/TAFFC.2017.2740923 450K manually annotated 1M+ images total
Abstract (summary): AffectNet is the largest available database of facial expressions in the wild, collected by querying six search engines using 1,250 emotion-related keywords in six languages. Of 1,250,000 images retrieved, 450,000 were manually annotated by expert annotators for the presence of eight discrete emotion categories. The paper also proposes a deep neural network (DNN) baseline for both discrete and continuous emotion prediction.

Inter-annotator Agreement (Table 6, footnote)
The paper reports 60.7% agreement between two independent annotators across all 8 classes, measured using Cohen's Kappa. This is the ground-truth validation cited in Paper 1 of EVAtAR-IDEA. DeepFace (using AffectNet weights) achieves 0.64 normalised F1 on this same test set — marginally above human annotator agreement, confirming AffectNet as a credible scorer.
How EVAtAR-IDEA uses this paper
F(p) = 0.5·f1 + 0.3·f2 + 0.2·f3 where f1 = mean P(Happy) across frames — directly computed using AffectNet's Happy class (Class 3). The 60.7% inter-annotator agreement provides the validity floor: our DeepFace scorer is at least as reliable as human expert agreement on the same taxonomy. Pre-registered at OSF before any P1 data collection.
