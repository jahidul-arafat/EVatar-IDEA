THE STORY IN PLAIN TERMS

Evolutionary Computation has a 50-year head start. Since Holland formalised genetic algorithms in 1975, the EC research community has built a rich body of theory and practice — schema theorems, tournament selection, surrogate-assisted fitness, distributed meme spaces, variable-length genotype encodings, and interactive preference-based search. Dozier et al. applied this to facial expression in 2005, evolving an 11-integer emoticon vector using human preference across distributed users — the direct ancestor of this work. But every single one of these EC systems operated on the same category of object: synthetic, controlled, abstract representations — integer vectors, CPPN weights, binary strings. No EC researcher ever connected their optimiser to a live commercial AI video API, rendered actual avatar footage, or asked the question: can we automatically discover which expression instructions produce the most emotionally accurate video? The reason is structural — EC researchers do not have domain expertise in avatar video production, and until recently, no commercial platform exposed the kind of programmatic expression interface that would make such a connection tractable.

Commercial avatar platforms emerged between 2021 and 2024 and grew rapidly — but the academic research community has not kept pace. HeyGen Avatar IV exposes a symbolic expression tag system embedded directly in script text: tokens like [smile], [excited], and [serious] instruct the avatar to express a particular emotion during a particular phrase. D-ID offers natural-language style hints via API parameters. Akool derives expression entirely from speech audio prosody with no deliberate control interface at all. These are three fundamentally different expression architectures — yet not a single peer-reviewed study has ever measured expression accuracy across them, compared their outputs using an objective metric, or proposed a systematic method for deciding where, when, and which tags to use. Producers working with these platforms today operate entirely by intuition and trial-and-error. There is no published F(p) equivalent, no benchmark dataset, no guideline document, and no algorithm that can answer the question: given this sentence, which tag, placed where, for how long, will produce the emotion I intended? The gap is not a failure of the platforms — they expose what they expose. The gap is a failure of the research community to study those interfaces rigorously.

Aubria made this gap concrete. When the Auburn University AI team scripted their 18-slide avatar lecture, they placed over 200 expression tags by hand — spending days iterating, re-rendering, and guessing. The result was a 40–60% effective failure rate: tags ignored at sentence end, stacking collapse when three signals conflicted, temporal misalignment where expressions fired seconds after the emotional peak, semantic conflicts where positive content suppressed negative tags regardless of position, and platform-specific failures no one had documented. There was no measurement framework, no algorithm, and no prior work to consult. EVAtAR-IDEA is built precisely to close this gap. It is the first research programme positioned at the intersection of both fields: using an IDEA-inspired genetic algorithm — a direct extension of the supervisor's 2005 paper — to automatically discover which tag sequences produce the highest expression accuracy, as measured by DeepFace computer vision scoring, across three commercial avatar platforms. Prototype 1 establishes the first published measurement benchmark. Prototype 2 demonstrates that an evolutionary algorithm can outperform expert manual placement. Prototype 3 shows whether the advantage holds across platforms with fundamentally different control architectures. This is not a product evaluation. It is the first systematic, algorithm-driven study of expression tag optimisation in scripted avatar video generation — a contribution that neither field could have produced alone.

AT A GLANCE — KEY TAKEAWAYS
① EC researchers have 50 years of theory but have never optimised commercial AI video expression.

PRE-REGISTERED SUCCESS THRESHOLDS — DERIVATION & MOTIVATION
FITNESS FUNCTION
F(p) = 0.5f1 + 0.3f2 + 0.2f3
f1 · 50% weight
Emotion Accuracy
Mean P(target|frame) from DeepFace. Highest weight — the primary goal is the right emotion, everything else is secondary.
f2 · 30% weight
Temporal Alignment
Speed of correct onset. An expression that fires late is less useful than one that fires on time, even if eventually accurate.
f3 · 20% weight
Consistency
1 − std(frame probs). A flickering expression is worse than a held one. Lowest weight — nice to have, not primary.
Weights derived from a pedagogical avatar communication literature review (Mayer 2009 · Bailenson 2004) — emotional accuracy is dominant in learning retention, temporal precision is secondary.
≥0.75
F(p*) FITNESS SCORE
WHY 0.75?
DeepFace classification accuracy on AffectNet benchmarks: ~0.78 for clear, distinct expressions. A score of 0.75 means the avatar is producing an expression that CV systems can reliably detect — equivalent to "clearly readable" to a human viewer.
DERIVATION
0.0–0.3 = no signal · 0.3–0.5 = weak/ambiguous · 0.5–0.75 = partial · ≥0.75 = clear and reliable. Manual baseline expected ~0.35–0.55 from P1 data. GA must cross 0.75 to demonstrate practical utility.
<0.05
WILCOXON p-VALUE
WHY WILCOXON?
F(p) scores are not normally distributed — they are bounded [0,1] with a skewed distribution dominated by failures near 0. Wilcoxon signed-rank is the correct non-parametric paired test. Each sentence is paired: same sentence, GA score vs manual score.
WHY p<0.05?
Standard significance threshold. Pre-registering it prevents post-hoc p-hacking. With n=16 sentences, a paired Wilcoxon test at p<0.05 requires a consistent difference across most pairs — not just a few outliers.
≥0.5
COHEN'S d EFFECT SIZE
WHY EFFECT SIZE?
Statistical significance (p<0.05) alone is insufficient — with n=16 even a trivial difference can reach significance. Cohen's d measures practical magnitude. d≥0.5 = "medium effect" by Cohen's conventions — the improvement must be large enough to matter in practice, not just statistically detectable.
CONVENTION
d=0.2 small · d=0.5 medium ← threshold · d=0.8 large. A medium effect is the minimum that would change practitioner behaviour — below this, the improvement is statistically real but practically negligible.
WHY ALL THREE THRESHOLDS ARE REQUIRED SIMULTANEOUSLY
F(p*)≥0.75 alone — proves the GA found a good solution, but doesn't prove it's better than a human. The manual baseline might also be 0.75.
p<0.05 alone — proves GA is statistically better, but the difference could be d=0.1 — too small to justify using an algorithm in production.
d≥0.5 alone — proves large effect, but without the absolute threshold, a large improvement over a terrible baseline is still a terrible result.
All three together mean: the GA finds objectively good solutions (≥0.75), is reliably better than humans (p<0.05), and the improvement is large enough to matter in practice (d≥0.5). This is the gold standard for a falsifiable, pre-registered claim.

COMMITTEE Q — ARE WE OPTIMISING ALL 200+ TAGS?
WHAT "200+" MEANS
200+ is the total number of tag placements across Aubria's 18 slides — not 200 unique tags. The same small vocabulary, used repeatedly.
ACTUAL VOCABULARY
Only 21 tag tokens documented from production: 7 emotion · 8 gesture · 3 delivery · 3 timing variants. The search space is their ordering and placement — not 200 distinct tokens.
WHAT EACH PROTOTYPE DOES
P1 Measures all 200+ to establish baseline
P2 Optimises 16 sentences from 21-token vocab
P3 16 sentences × 3 platforms; finds rules
The goal is to discover placement rules that transfer — not to brute-force all 200+ placements. P2 optimises per sentence and stores winning sequences so future sentences of the same emotional type inherit the answer without re-rendering.

FULL LLD — 7-LAYER EVAtAR-IDEA ARCHITECTURE
LAYER-BY-LAYER EXPLANATION
L1 · SCRIPT INPUT + TAG VOCABULARY
Human labels each sentence with one target emotion (excited/serious/curious/surprised). 21-token vocabulary: 7 emotion, 8 gesture, 3 delivery, 3 timing. Meme space queried by emotion_category — if prior solutions exist, pop[:3] are seeded from them.
L2 · CANDIDATE SOLUTION REPRESENTATION
p = [(pos, tag, dur)] — variable-length ordered list. pos = word index, tag = 21-vocab token, dur = duration seconds. Converted to HeyGen inline script before render. Constraint repair: max 1 emotion/3 words, no stacking, sentiment check, max 3 tags per sentence.
L3 · RENDER API + CACHE
POST /v2/video/generate → poll until completed → download 4s MP4. 5 parallel async aiohttp jobs. Cache: hash(script+tags) → ~60% hit rate → ~1,100 renders skipped → ~$82 saved. Total: ~1,840 unique renders × $0.067 ≈ $123.
L4 · DEEPFACE MEASUREMENT
OpenCV 25fps → 100 frames per clip. DeepFace AffectNet: per-frame 7-class emotion probability. f1=mean P(target), f2=onset speed, f3=1−std. F(p) = 0.5f1 + 0.3f2 + 0.2f3. Replaces Dozier 2005 human preference loop entirely.
L5 · DEAP GA ENGINE
DEAP 1.4.1: Individual=[(pos,tag,dur)]. Tournament k=3. 5 custom operators. No crossover (discrete sequences). 8 generations × 3 independent runs. Best p* selected across all runs. Wilcoxon p<0.05 + Cohen's d≥0.5 are final success criteria.
L6 · MEME SPACE + STATS
JSON keyed by emotion_category. Store if F(p*)>0.70 — top-5 per key. Seeds pop[:3] for similar future sentences. SciPy: Wilcoxon signed-rank (P2 paired), Mann-Whitney U (P3 cross-platform), Cohen's d. Matplotlib: convergence curves per sentence.
L7 · OUTPUT p*
Optimised tag sequence verified at F(p*)≥0.75. Feeds P3 cross-platform comparison (HeyGen · D-ID · Akool). Stored in meme space. Released as open dataset. Forms basis of Paper 2 (IEEE Access) and Paper 3 (Expert Systems).

Threats to ValidityInternal · External · Platform · Measurement · Perception

Answering the Committee's Hardest Questions First.

Every PhD committee asks about limitations. These are not weaknesses — they are boundaries that define where the research claims apply and where it doesn't.

Internal Validity
DeepFace Accuracy Limits
DeepFace performs well on strong expressions (excited, happy, surprised) but produces near-zero signal on subtle expressions ([curious], [thoughtful]). These are the weakest tags in HeyGen's library — and DeepFace's detection limit coincides with the tag's amplitude limit.
MITIGATION
Documented as limitation. GA may discover [surprised] as a higher-amplitude proxy for [curious]. change_tag operator explicitly tests substitutes. F8 failure mode directly addresses this.
External Validity
Single Avatar, Single Domain
All experiments use the Aubria face model and a single academic presentation script domain. Results may not generalise directly to other avatar face models (different facial geometry = different tag response), different content domains (marketing vs education vs news), or different languages (non-English script prosody).
BOUNDARY CLAIM
Claims are bounded to: Aubria face model, English-language academic presentation scripts, HeyGen Avatar IV. Generalisation is an explicit future work direction — not a deficiency of this study.
Platform Validity
HeyGen Avatar IV May Update
HeyGen updates its Avatar IV engine periodically. A model update between P1 data collection and P2 GA evolution could change tag behaviour, invalidating the P1 baseline as a benchmark for P2 results. API version changes could also break integration mid-experiment.
MITIGATION
Pin API version at M1 start. Record avatar_id, voice_id, and API version in every experiment log. P1 and P2 conducted in consecutive months M1–M8 to minimise platform change exposure.
Measurement Validity
CV Score ≠ Human Perception
F(p) measures computer vision probability — not human perceptual experience. A viewer might perceive a subtle expression as appropriate and meaningful even when DeepFace returns low confidence. The algorithm optimises for what a neural model detects, not what a human feels. These may correlate but are not identical.
POST-GRAD EXTENSION
Human perception study (A/B rating) is explicitly scoped as a post-graduation extension. This PhD establishes the automated CV baseline first — human correlation study follows as Paper 4.
Construct Validity
Akool P3 is a Lower Bound
Akool Avatar Video has zero deliberate expression control — it derives expression entirely from audio prosody. The Akool condition in P3 is not a failure of the platform; it is a deliberate lower bound. A low Akool F(p) confirms that deliberate control matters. A high Akool F(p) would be the more surprising finding.
FRAMING
P3 is not designed to make Akool look bad — it is designed to quantify what deliberate control contributes. Both outcomes (control matters / doesn't matter) are scientifically interesting and publishable.
Ecological Validity
4-Second Clips vs Full Videos
P2 evolution uses 4-second clips for efficiency. Real avatar videos are 2–5 minutes long. A tag optimised for a 4-second measurement window may behave differently in a longer continuous script — carry-over effects (F4 failure mode) only emerge across sentence boundaries, not within a 4-second clip.
MITIGATION
P3 uses 6-second clips (full natural sentence length) to capture cross-boundary effects. F4 carry-over constraint is enforced in the constraint repair handler regardless of clip length.

One Paragraph.
The Complete Picture.

The rapid growth of scripted avatar video generation has created a critical but unaddressed production bottleneck: despite offering expression control interfaces ranging from HeyGen's symbolic tag system to auto-expression engines requiring no user input, no scientific method exists for determining which control signals to use, where to place them, or in what combination — leaving every practitioner reliant entirely on manual trial-and-error. EVAtAR-IDEA addresses this by extending Dozier et al. (2005) IDEA — replacing human preference fitness with automated DeepFace CV scoring, replacing real-valued emoticon vectors with variable-length ordered discrete tag sequences, and replacing recency-based meme space with emotion-category-keyed knowledge store — producing: (1) first published benchmark F(p) for expression quality, (2) GA-evolved tag optimisation pipeline reducing expert production from days to overnight, and (3) first controlled cross-platform comparison of expression accuracy across HeyGen, D-ID, and Akool.
